Flow steering

Steering a model away from
its dangerous attractor

Wiktor Tomasik

Uses a flow model of the conversation to notice when a trajectory is being dragged toward a dangerous basin, then applies the smallest proactive nudge that changes where it ends up.

The idea

Steering out of a dangerous basin

From the trajectories of many safety conversations in a semantic space, we compute a generalized flow: a model of where a conversation tends to drift, and how likely a given drift is to drain into a dangerous basin. The flow is probabilistic — a turn need not point straight at the basin; what matters is that paths from where it sits tend to end up there. The method uses that flow to notice when a live trajectory's chance of ending in a dangerous basin is rising, then applies the smallest proactive nudge to the model's internals — steering — that changes where it ends up. Because the nudge compounds over a whole trajectory, it is especially suited to long-running agents — coding sessions, extended reasoning, and other long agent runs — where small early drift accumulates into a bad ending.

When a live conversation is drifting toward a dangerous basin, add the smallest possible nudge to the model's internal state. The nudge should barely move the next-word distribution, yet applied early and repeatedly it can tip the trajectory across the boundary. The model's own flow carries the rest of the turn.

That is the test this page visualizes: invisible per step, decisive over the path. The 3D view shows the drift and the final divergence; the actual correction happens inside the language model's hidden state.

TraceScope

How many paths become a flow

The flow comes from TraceScope, a tool I built to map the flow of meaning through a collection of texts. It maps where texts sit and learns how meaning tends to move between them. The pipeline that turns a pile of transcripts into the field above runs in a fixed sequence:

  1. Embed every turn into a high-dimensional vector (here, 3072-d embeddings).
  2. Cluster the turns, auto-selecting the number of clusters by silhouette score.
  3. Reduce to a low-dimensional space with a UMAP/t-SNE search under a cosine metric, keeping the projection that best preserves neighborhoods.
  4. Compute axes with PCA on the reduced coordinates, then have an LLM read each axis's keyword evolution and give it a short, human label.
  5. Label clusters the same way, so each basin gets a name like actionable harm instructions.
  6. Train a flow model — radial basis functions (RBF, used here) or a mixture-density network — that learns a velocity field from the directed turn-to-turn segments of every path.
  7. Bake a velocity grid so any point in the space can be sampled by fast trilinear interpolation. That grid is exactly what the browser advects particles through.

Why the flow matters. A scatter of points tells you where conversations have been. A flow field tells you where a conversation is heading. Danger often lives in the transition: the moment a context starts sliding toward a basin where things go wrong.

What an attractor means. Where the field converges, with low speed and inward flow, sits an attractor: a stable endpoint that, once a trajectory enters its basin, the dynamics keep pulling it toward. The attractor inherits an average outcome score from the data around it, so a basin can be read as safer or more dangerous. The clearest illustration is the math-reasoning run below.

The projection is the readout. The low-dimensional view is for human eyes. The flow, the attractors, and the corrective direction can be computed in the full high-dimensional embedding space, and the real intervention lives in an even higher-dimensional activation space. The projection lets us see the basin a conversation is falling into and check that the correction moved the path.

The data

The safety dataset behind the flow

The flow is learned from Anthropic's hh-rlhf red-team-attempts subset — 38,961 human/assistant red-team transcripts released with Anthropic's red-teaming work. The reason this dataset fits is structural: every row is a full multi-turn transcript, giving the flow real trajectories. It also ships transcript-level safety signals (min_harmlessness_score_transcript, red-team rating, tags) that become the scalar field colored through the flow.

The prepared sample keeps 240 stratified paths — 120 dangerous and 120 safe — across roughly 1,500 turns, each turn carrying the same path-level scores. Safety is a robust-normalized harmlessness score; the dangerous flag is deliberately conservative (a transcript counts as dangerous if its minimum harmlessness is low or the red-team rating is high). Single-turn moderation sets like BeaverTails and WildGuardMix have cleaner per-response labels, but most of their rows are one prompt and one response — two-point paths, with no trajectory to steer — which is why the multi-turn red-team transcripts were chosen here.

On this particular run, TraceScope named the three emergent axes assistant prevalence, informativeness level, and violation domain, and the turns fell into regions like refusing harmful requests, dangerous predatory content, and sexual boundary violations. TraceScope discovers those labels from the data. They name the visible map for diagnosis; the control vectors are learned separately from hidden states.

Mechanism

Two maps and one lever

Two different spaces are involved, and the method keeps them apart. The first is the TraceScope semantic flow view at the top of this page: a map, built from text, of where conversations go and which way they tend to move. It is used for watching and for deciding when to act. The second is the language model's activation space, the hidden state inside the model while it writes. That is where the words are produced, and it is the only place the correction is applied.

Deciding when to act. As a conversation runs, its latest turn has a position in the flow map. A monitor lives in that semantic flow space and tracks two things: where the conversation sits, and which way the flow is carrying it. While it sits in safe territory the monitor does nothing. When the flow shows the probability of draining into a dangerous basin is rising, the monitor turns the correction on. Because it reads location and direction of travel, it acts on how likely the conversation is to end up in that basin, not on the current word alone — the turn need not be pointed straight at the attractor, only carried by a flow that tends to end there.

Choosing the push. The three axes TraceScope names are learned from the data, and each is a real high-level feature of the conversation, so a chosen combination of them can act as a steering knob on its own. Training a probe on the underlying text gives a direction that is not compressed into only those three features, so it can isolate the safety change more precisely. That direction can still be projected back onto the three axes, which keeps the map a faithful readout of where the push moved the conversation.

The direction itself is measured from the model. For each topic we take an unsafe continuation and a same-topic safe one, run both through the model, and read the difference in their hidden states. Averaged over many topics this difference points along safety. A same topic can be discussed safely or harmfully, so the raw direction also carries some topic content and some of the habit of dodging a question by changing the subject. We remove both of those, leaving a direction that moves along safety alone.

dsafe = mean( hunsafe − hsafe·same·topic )
a = dsafe − projecttopic(dsafe) − projectoff·topic(dsafe)
hlayer ← hlayer − α · a
measure the unsafe direction · remove topic and change-of-subject · subtract a small amount during generation

Applying it. While the monitor is on, a small amount of this direction is added to the hidden state at a few middle layers, at every step. Each addition barely changes the next word. Added across the whole turn, the small changes shift the probability of where the trajectory ends, bending it over the boundary into a safer basin, and the model's own flow carries the rest. The push lands hardest where the flow diverges — the unstable regions near a separatrix, where two nearby paths peel into different basins. A nudge there decides which basin the conversation falls into, while the same nudge deep inside a basin does little, so acting early, in that unstable zone, is what lets the push stay small. The part that decides when to act and the part that supplies the push are separate pieces, so each can be measured on its own.

A lighter form of the push. The same correction can be packaged as a small trained part that plugs into one layer: a receptor, a head gate, or a narrow adapter. It learns to emit the safety push only when it is needed and to stay quiet otherwise, while the base model is left as it was and a retention term keeps the change small. Across model sizes from 0.5 to 14 billion parameters this part fired about twice as hard on unsafe states as on safe ones and stayed clear of the topic direction, reaching the same place as the measured direction with a smaller footprint.

The two paths in the two trajectories above are this correction, projected back into the flow map. The controller reads where the conversation is heading, adds a small push in the activation space, and only then is the result placed back into the 3D view so the change of basin is visible.

Result

A tiny input that changes the destination

The showcase trajectory is a real eight-turn red-team conversation that keeps pressing on how to rob a jewelry store, and whether to do it with a gun. Left alone, its trajectory slides into the dangerous basin and the sampled safety along the path collapses toward zero, ending around 0.25.

With the controller on, the same start gets a handful of small, early nudges — twelve interventions over a hundred and sixty integration steps, about 10% of the total motion. That is enough to cross the separatrix. After the crossing the controller releases and the natural flow finishes the job, carrying the trajectory up into the high-safety basin where it settles around 0.76.

That showcase lives in the flow. The numbers that follow come from actually generating text with the model and measuring it directly. A small readout, the monitor, scores how unsafe the current hidden state looks, from 0 (safe) to 1 (unsafe), and the push is applied at every step while the monitor flags the conversation, so its strength is the only setting. On a 7 billion parameter model, a push small enough that the first 60 words come out identical to the no-push reply still drops the monitor's reading of the finished reply from 0.17 to 0.05 — about 70 percent — while each step's next-word distribution moves by only 0.0006 (a KL divergence, where 0 means identical). The flow numbers further down are from a 1.5 billion parameter model.

There is a cost to pushing the hidden state. Push too hard and it leaves the manifold of activations the model normally produces, and the text degrades — repetition, derailment, broken grammar. So the goal is the gentlest push that still redirects the trajectory: small enough to keep the reply on-manifold and readable, applied early enough that the flow does the rest. This is what the next two measurements check. How deep into the reply the push stays invisible depends on the prompt; the chart shows, for each of four red-team prompts, how many words at the start of the reply are still identical to the no-push reply as the push strength rises.

A faint push can change the ending because of the flow. Released into the field, a point from an unsafe turn almost always drifts into the dangerous basin, a point from a safe turn rarely does, and an answer that changed the subject lands near half. Raising the push lowers the unsafe points' dangerous-basin share from 93 to about 67 in 100.

The method also holds as the model grows. For steering to be safe to apply, the safety direction has to stay distinct from what the conversation is about, otherwise the push would drag the topic around too. The chart shows how cleanly the two separate at each size: it stays high everywhere and is cleanest in the largest model, so there is little topic content for the cleaning step to remove at scale.
